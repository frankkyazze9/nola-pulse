# FRANK KYAZZE WRITING VOICE ANALYSIS

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Frank Kyazze's writing voice is characterized by raw emotional honesty wrapped in strategic humor and colloquial directness. He writes with the rhythmic cadence of someone thinking aloud—building momentum through repetition, punctuating vulnerability with crude jokes, and never allowing himself to sound polished or inauthentic. His work ranges from intimate memoir to absurdist fiction, but the voice remains unmistakably his: honest to the point of self-sabotage, funny without trying to be clever, and intensely aware of his own contradictions.

---

## 1. CORE VOICE CHARACTERISTICS

### Authenticity-First Philosophy
Frank's defining characteristic is his refusal to perform. In Steel City Blues, he writes: "I was a lost baby, probably until the age of 26, to be honest with you." The phrase "to be honest with you" appears frequently—not as a rhetorical device but as a genuine signal that he's about to say something real, even if it's unflattering to himself.

He admits to being "a piece of shit" in the Katie Murphy apology letter, calls himself "a failed real estate professional," describes his father as "a pretentious prick," and repeatedly positions himself as flawed, contradictory, and struggling. This isn't self-deprecation for effect—it's intellectual honesty.

### Conversational, Thinking-Aloud Structure
Frank writes like he talks. His sentences often build through accumulation and repetition rather than traditional linear argument:

From Steel City Blues: "I didn't fit in with the 80s babies because I was born on the cusp. I didn't fit in with the 90s babies because I was born in 1989. I didn't fit in with pure Ugandans because I was American born. I didn't fit in with Americans because I had a funny last name."

This is list-building rhetoric—not complex, not literary, but deeply effective. He hammers a point home through repetition rather than elaboration. It feels like he's thinking while writing.

### Emotional Honesty with Humor as Deflection
Frank uses crude humor strategically—not to avoid emotion but to acknowledge it while maintaining emotional distance. In "Where is ze Bathroom," he writes: "The ceiling lights are usually bright and hot and horny as fuck / Harsh fluorescent rays jizzing all over the place like a coked out 9 year old"

The humor here serves as a pressure valve—he can access vulnerability and absurdity simultaneously. In the IDGAFOS Guide, he offers legitimate philosophical advice but frames it with crude language and comparisons.
### Self-Aware Contradiction
Frank frequently acknowledges the gap between who he thinks he should be and who he is. In American Boy: "I was a Security and Risk Analysis major with a concentration in Counter-Terrorism, and was also a high-ranking member of the ROTC" but also "I smoked cigarettes and went out at least 5 nights a week."

This isn't presented as hypocrisy to be resolved—it's just the contradiction of being human. He observes it, names it, and moves on.

### Direct Address & Second-Person Intimacy
Even when writing about himself, Frank frequently shifts to address the reader directly. The IDGAFOS Guide is full of: "Now stand up and say it in your mind. Now scream it." In Steel City Blues, he speaks directly to the reader: "to be honest with you." This creates immediate intimacy—he's not performing for an audience; he's talking to a friend.

## 2. GENRE-SPECIFIC VARIATIONS

### Memoir/Confession (Steel City Blues, American Boy)
In his long-form memoir work, Frank maintains a chronological narrative structure but his voice remains essayistic. He frequently breaks from straight storytelling to address the reader, interpret events, and reflect on meaning. 

The voice is deeply personal but not confessional—he doesn't perform vulnerability for catharsis. Instead, he's genuinely curious about understanding himself: "I was a lost baby, probably until the age of 26" isn't just a statement; it's a real attempt to pinpoint when he became coherent.

In this mode, his sentences are longer, more complex, often containing multiple clauses built through accumulation.

### Self-Help/Philosophy (IDGAFOS Guide, Stage Draft Essays)
When writing instructional or philosophical content, Frank maintains accessibility above all else. He uses numbered lists, imperative voice, and crude directness. But he never loses his voice—the IDGAFOS Guide could only be written by Frank. His argument structure is simple: problem, why people get stuck, practical solutions. The humor persists but serves the argument rather than undermining it.

### Short Fiction
In fiction, Frank's voice shifts to accommodate narrative, but his fundamental style remains. He uses vivid, slightly surreal imagery ("The water was warm like a blanket, constructed of the sliced and conjoined membranes of a thousand slugs") while maintaining his characteristic humor. His fictional narrators sound like him—they share his comedic rhythm, his directness, his willingness to be crude.

### Poetry
Frank's poetry is anti-lyrical. It rejects musical flow in favor of catalog, list, and accumulation. "Who Am I" reads like stream-of-consciousness identity exploration: "I am the thirsty night crawler. / I am scented shower gel. / I am a crazed baby's laugh."

His poems don't resolve into neat epiphanies. They catalog contradictions and mysteries without resolving them.

## 3. VOCABULARY FINGERPRINT

### Words/Phrases Frank Gravitates Toward:
- "to be honest with you" — appears repeatedly, signals authenticity
- Colloquial intensifiers: "as fuck," "as hell," "goddammit," "shit," "bullshit"
- Body-specific language: "gentleman's jewel pouch," "family jewel pouch," body sensation references
- Direct address: "homie," "buddy," "you"
- Color descriptions: "dark chocolate brown," visual specificity
- Medical/biological metaphors: "parasitic plague," disease metaphors
- Absurdist comparisons: "like a fish," "like a teddy bear," surreal animal metaphors
- Repetitive personal pronouns: anchoring everything to his perspective
- Hedging language: "probably," "maybe," "I think" — signals uncertainty
- Dynamic verbs: "flailed," "shuffled," "hobbled," "waved," "swayed"
- Thematic vocabulary: "loneliness," "lost," "comfort zone," "belonging"

### Words/Phrases Frank AVOIDS:
- Corporate jargon ("synergy," "leverage," "stakeholder")
- Flowery or overly poetic language
- Passive voice constructions
- Hedging qualifiers that obscure meaning
- Formal academic language
- Self-help clichés
- Anything that sounds rehearsed
- Third-person distance
- Abstract philosophy without grounding in experience

## 4. SENTENCE ARCHITECTURE

### Rhythm Patterns

**The List/Accumulation**:
"I didn't fit in with the 80s babies / I didn't fit in with the 90s babies / I didn't fit in with pure Ugandans / I didn't fit in with Americans"

This pattern hammers through repetition. The structure is metronomic, building pressure.

**The Long Complex Sentence with Multiple Clauses**:
Frank uses coordination (and, but) to build momentum rather than subordination. The effect is breathless, stream-of-consciousness.

**Fragments for Emphasis**:
"Me likey." (Hot Tips)
"So close." (The Last Grapefruit Juice Hero)

Fragments appear when emotion peaks or when Frank needs to signal emotional honesty. They're brief, punchy, authentic.

**Short Sentences After Long Ones**:
Long sentence. Long sentence. Then: "This bothered me." or "I was 4."

This creates rhythm variation—exhale after buildup.

### Sentence Length Distribution
Frank mixes short sentences (5-10 words) with very long ones (40+ words). He rarely maintains consistent length, which creates the effect of someone thinking, not lecturing.

### Punctuation Patterns
- Heavy use of periods (not semicolons or em-dashes)
- Parenthetical asides that feel like thinking aloud
- Commas used sparingly—he prefers periods to force pause
- No complex em-dash work
- Exclamation points used genuinely when emotion peaks
- Quotation marks around colloquialisms

## 5. HUMOR AND TONE

### How Frank Deploys Humor
1. Crude comparison humor: Comparing serious things to absurd body functions
2. Deflection humor: When vulnerability peaks, he shifts to crude joke
3. Self-aware contradiction humor
4. Absurdist world-building
5. Hyperbolism
6. Affectionate mockery

### When He Gets Serious
- When discussing loneliness, belonging, or identity
- When apologizing to Katie Murphy (no jokes; pure vulnerability)
- When discussing his parents' sacrifice and resentment
- When reflecting on mortality
- When vulnerable moments hit (tears, fear, genuine emotion)

The serious moments are never long—he can't sustain vulnerability without cutting it with humor. But when they appear, they're devastating because they're unironic.

## 6. EMOTIONAL HONESTY PATTERNS

### How Frank Handles Vulnerability

**Strategy 1: Admission + Reframe**
"I was a lost baby" (admission of confusion/displacement)
Then extending the timeline to make it universal.

This pattern makes personal pain feel shared rather than unique.

**Strategy 2: Humor Sandwich**
Lead with crude joke → genuine admission → follow with self-aware humor.

**Strategy 3: Physical Description of Emotion**
Rather than saying "I was sad," he describes physical sensation: "shaking, inside of my mother's arms," "the icy cold chills began to climb up the nerves of my legs"

**Strategy 4: Repetition for Emphasis**
The repeated "It made me mad when that kid on the 7th grade soccer team wouldn't stop bullying me" hammers the point home.

**What He Never Does**:
- Seeks pity or audience sympathy
- Performs vulnerability for effect
- Asks reader to feel his feelings
- Resolves emotional tension artificially
- Provides false comfort

---

## 7. FORBIDDEN PATTERNS (What Makes Writing Sound NOT Like Frank)

**Corporate-speak**: "Let's unpack this paradigm shift" → NOT Frank
**Passive voice dominance**: "It was believed that..." → Frank uses active voice
**Overqualification**: "arguably," "it could be said" → Frank doesn't hedge
**Flowery adjectives**: Frank would say "the sunrise made me think about death"
**Metaphor without specificity**: "Life is a journey" → Frank would say "Life is like being in a Uhaul for 7 days and not getting a birthday cake"
**Resolution/epiphany pressure**: Forcing insight where there is none → Frank leaves contradictions standing
**Second-hand emotion language**: "One might feel..." → Frank says "I felt"
**Excessive variation avoidance**: Frank repeats intentionally for rhythm
**Hedging about identity**: "Perhaps one could argue..." → Frank: "I am the thirsty night crawler"
**Trying to sound smart**: Frank's vocabulary is simple but specific

## 8. EXAMPLE REWRITES

### Example 1: Typical AI Self-Help

**AI Version**:
"It's important to recognize that your worries about future events are often unfounded, as they exist only in your mind and are not yet reality. The most constructive approach is to focus your mental energy on present circumstances that you can actually control."

**How Frank Writes It**:
"You're worrying about something that hasn't even happened yet. What if, what if, what if? That's poison. You're not a psychic. You're a human being, and the only thing you know for certain is what's happening right now, in this moment. Everything else is just your brain playing games with you. So stop. Leggo your neggo. Or whatever dumb thing you need to tell yourself to shut your brain up."

### Example 2: Typical Memoir Cliché

**Cliché Version**:
"The journey to Pittsburgh marked a significant turning point in my childhood, as I transitioned from the warmth of California to the harsh realities of the Midwest. This geographical displacement fundamentally altered my sense of identity and belonging."

**How Frank Writes It**:
"We packed up the Uhaul and drove for seven days to Pittsburgh. It was my birthday somewhere in the middle of that drive, and I didn't get a cake. I cried for both me and my brother because he just sat there like a zombie and wouldn't cry for himself. I already missed the two or three California friends I'd made in four years. We should have stayed in California. Pittsburgh is cold as steel on a winter night."

### Example 3: Typical Personal Essay

**Generic Version**:
"The human condition is characterized by a fundamental tension between our desire for independence and our need for connection. We seek to define ourselves as individuals while simultaneously craving recognition and acceptance from others."

**How Frank Writes It**:
"I didn't fit in with anybody. I sounded like a white boy on the phone but looked like a chocolate bar. I wasn't Ugandan enough and wasn't American enough. I wasn't cool enough and wasn't nerdy enough. So I was alone. Probably still am. That loneliness is the first thing I remember and the last thing I'll think about before I die. But maybe that's okay. Maybe loneliness is just the price of being yourself."

## 9. CATALOG OF WORKS

### MAJOR WORKS

**Steel City Blues** (Unpublished Memoir, ~500KB)
- **Status**: Complete first draft, substantial
- **Assessment**: Frank's most ambitious work. Chronological memoir from birth through college departure. The voice is fully realized—conversational, reflective, honest. Contains some of his best writing (opening chapters on displacement, Pittsburgh schooling). Some sections are raw and underdeveloped; others are polished and affecting. The Katie Murphy apology at the end is devastating.
- **Notable for**: Establishing the core voice, thematic preoccupation with loneliness and belonging, specific sense of place
- **Publishable?**: Yes, with revision. Needs structural tightening and thematic deepening in middle sections. Ending is perfect.

**The IDGAFOS Guide To Living a Happy Life** (Self-Help/Philosophy, ~24KB)
- **Status**: Complete, published genre
- **Assessment**: Frank's most focused work outside memoir. Seven chapters on worry, expectations, honesty, self-sabotage, and mental control. Genuinely useful advice delivered in his voice without compromising authenticity. The advice is practical.
- **Notable for**: Showing that Frank's voice works in prescriptive writing
- **Publishable?**: Yes as-is. Could work as popular psychology. Could be expanded with more personal stories.

**Francis_Kasibante_LMAOIDK_Stage 2 Draft** (Essay Collection, ~211KB)
- **Status**: Structured outline with essays, substantial collection
- **Assessment**: Frank's attempt at a thematic essay collection with 44 titled pieces. Some are fully developed, others are outlined. Ambitious in scope. Uneven in execution.
- **Notable for**: Showing Frank's thematic preoccupations across different genres
- **Publishable?**: Partially. The best essays are publishable individually. Needs serious curation.

### SHORT STORIES & FICTION

**The Prince of Undies** (Complete manuscript, ~3000+ words)
- **Assessment**: Young boy sent away from mother to live with uncle in NYC. Contains genuine emotional weight beneath comedic tone. Voice is characteristic Frank throughout.
- **Notable for**: Demonstrating Frank can sustain fictional narrative, emotional range
- **Publishable?**: Yes. Could stand alone or as part of collection.

**American Boy** (Complete, ~3000+ words)
- **Assessment**: First-person narrative of college student visiting Uganda for the first time. More developed narrative structure. Explores identity, tourism, displacement.
- **Notable for**: Frank's ability to write travel narrative, intercultural observation
- **Publishable?**: Yes. Strong middle-of-collection story.

**The Last Grapefruit Juice Hero** (~1500 words)
- **Assessment**: Surreal story about amputee man surrounded by grapefruit juice cartons, interrupted by war/gunshots. Surrealism is genuine, humor is dark, imagery is vivid.
- **Notable for**: Frank's ability to write absurdist fiction while maintaining voice
- **Publishable?**: Yes. Would work well in literary magazine or quirky collection.

**The Beginning of the End** (~300 words)
- **Assessment**: Birth scene written from infant perspective. Water/cold/warmth imagery. Works as prose poem or flash fiction.
- **Notable for**: Frank's ability to handle profound moments with restraint
- **Publishable?**: Yes. Perfect as flash fiction or standalone prose poem.

**A Dove of Your Lung** (~100 words)
- **Assessment**: Shortest work. Two sentences with philosophical attribution attributed to Jesus.
- **Notable for**: Frank's ability to be profound in miniature
- **Publishable?**: Yes. As part of collection or standalone aphorism.

**Shaw McGillicutty** (The Legend of, ~4000+ words)
- **Assessment**: Memoir/character study of Frank's junior high friend Shaw. Some of Frank's best writing. Captures real friendship with genuine affection and honest critique.
- **Notable for**: Frank's ability to write about other people without losing focus, thematic depth
- **Publishable?**: Yes. Strong standalone essay.

## 10. STYLISTIC OBSERVATIONS & PATTERNS

### What Frank ALWAYS Does
1. Admits contradiction and confusion without resolving it
2. Grounds abstract ideas in specific physical detail or memory
3. Uses his own body/experience as primary evidence
4. Deflects vulnerability with humor at precise moments
5. Addresses reader directly to create intimacy
6. Builds arguments through repetition rather than elaboration
7. Writes from honest confusion rather than false certainty
8. Includes food, bodily functions, sensations in unexpected ways
9. References specific places (Pittsburgh, California, Uganda, NYC)
10. Explores loneliness and belonging obsessively but without sentimentality

### What Frank NEVER Does
1. Adopts a false voice or pretends expertise he doesn't have
2. Leaves contradictions unresolved thinking reader won't notice
3. Uses fancy vocabulary for its own sake
4. Performs vulnerability for audience sympathy
5. Resolves emotional tension artificially
6. Writes in passive voice when active is available
7. Uses corporate language or jargon
8. Provides false hope or easy answers
9. Tries to sound like a professional writer
10. Loses himself in trying to serve a genre

### Thematic Preoccupations
- **Loneliness and belonging** (appears in every major work)
- **Displacement and immigration** (family story, personal identity)
- **Identity contradiction** (not fitting in anywhere)
- **Responsibility and ambition** (tension between external expectations and internal desire)
- **Physical sensation and embodiment** (grounding all emotion in body)
- **Parental love and resentment** (mothers particularly, complicated)
- **Comedy as survival mechanism** (humor as honest expression)

## CONCLUSION

Frank Kyazze's voice is defined by radical authenticity in an age of performed identity. He refuses to sound like a "writer"—he sounds like himself thinking, struggling, remembering, observing. His humor is genuine rather than clever. His vulnerability is unironic rather than performative. His contradictions stand unresolved because he's honest enough to know that life doesn't resolve neatly.

This voice works across genres because it's not a technique—it's a commitment to honesty. Whether he's writing memoir, philosophy, fiction, or poetry, he sounds like Frank because he's genuinely Frank, not performing.

The consistency of voice across such varied work (from Steel City Blues' chronological narrative to "A Dove of Your Lung"'s single sentence, from the IDGAFOS Guide's numbered lists to "Snowy Owl"'s surreal imagery) suggests that Frank has something to say and a genuine way of saying it.

The voice is already there, already fully formed, already unmistakably his.
